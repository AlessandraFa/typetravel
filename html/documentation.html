What is Typetravel?

Typetravel [Name: TYPE-TRAVEL: refers to the fact that the website allows time-travel in the domain of typography (and layout): the user can travel in time by changing the different typographical styles and layout inspired to different periods - from the past to the future. The developers of the website paid particular attention to visual details by studying the features of the different styles selected. One style from older age (Renaissance style, 1450-1550 - focus on Italy), one from the early XX century (Secession - focus on the Viennese version), one from contemporary age (after 1945 - 1980s style) and one style reflecting our vision of webdesign in the near future - based on minimalism, interactivity (?), dynamicity]

The Renaissance style refers to the style of typography and art of the 16th century. It imitates the harmonic disposition of text on the page and aims at reproducing the feeling of reading the page of a printed book from early italian printing offices.

FONTS

The font Bembo was chosen for this style, a serif typeface based on the roman style designed by Francesco Griffo for Venetian printer Aldo Manuzio.

The name "Bembo" refers to the first publication with the font, the book Petri Bembi de Aetna Angelum Chabrielem liber, usually referred to as De Aetna, by Italian humanist poet Pietro Bembo (Figure X). This font was thus chosen among several others, even if other fonts such as Garamond were considered due to the similarity with the font the original source De Aetna. This is due to the fact that the font Bembo was an inspiration for many typographers, together with other roman fonts designed by the typographers of the Aldine Press.

As one could notice by observing the images below, this is not the original version of the font, but the one redesigned by Monotype in 1990. It is possible to notice some differences between the original version and the one we use today, such as the use of the "long" s (ſ), the several ligatures (s and t, c and t), or symbols that are fallen into disuse (such as ꝗ).

TEXT & LAYOUT

As visible in the image above (Figure X), the text of the articles on this website is justified and spacing on the sides was added to imitate the page structure of renaissance printed books, but still guaranteeing a pleasent reading experience. Differently from today, in early printings text content was justified manually by adding or removing spaces when necessary, so that it is possible to observe some peculiarities in the original documents, such as the missing spaces after punctuation. However, these details were not reproduced in order to enhance readability.

Further details in the text were introduced with the aim of imitating early printings. Titles are written in uppercase letters - which in 1500 printings had the purpose of imitating Roman Epigraphy - and multi-lines titles alternate smaller and larger font-sizes, both typical traits of Renaissance book titles (Figure X). Furthermore, the increased letter spacing in the title is a further feature inspired by original prints (Figure X).

Figure X: Vignola, Le Regole de’ cinque ordini [...], Palladio, L’antichità dell’alma città di Roma [...]

The introductory paragraphs below the title were shaped as inverted triangles. This was a greatly widespread layout for titlepages of Renaissance printings and can be observed in many documents of the time(Figure X).

Figure X: Poliphilus: 1 (left), 2 (right)

Spacing between text lines was set to 1 to imitate orginal printed pages, in particular Pietro Bembo's work De Aetna, as shown by Peter Burnhill in his work "Type spaces. In-house norms in the typography of Aldus Manutius" (page 8, page 68 -FIG NUMX).

Figure X: Burnhill, Type spaces, p. 43.

COLOR SCHEME

The colors used for the Renaissance style aimed at reproducing dark printing ink on a white-light grey page (here, we refer only to the issue section). The color selected for the text content is #424242, a dark gray which was considered more suitable to represent ink, which does not appear black on the page. The color #F2F2F2 was used for the articles background. It is grayis white that contrasts with the dark grey of the text. During the design process, the option of using a color tending to yellow was considered, but the color #F2F2F2 was finally selected with the intention of bringing back the user to 1500 by reproducing the semblance of Renaissance book pages at the time of the printing, instead of emphasizing the signs of time on the paper. This choice also allowed us to draw a clear distinction between the Renaissance and Secession themes of the website, as the background color selected for the latter is a linen-white. The navbar and side decorations present darker colors than the text background, respectively #C0C0C0 and #E5E5E5, with the aim of framing the text content with darker elements. The second style developed for this website is based on the work of the artistic movement of the Viennese Secession. In particular, it draws inspiration from the Austrian magazine Ver Sacrum, founded in Vienna by secessionist artists Gustav Klimt and Max Kurzweil and published from 1898 to 1902. The magazine aimed at spreading the new artistic style through the publication of illustrations and decorative elements and the characteristic layout design.

The Vienna Secession can be referred to as the Austrian version of Jugendstil, the German term for Art Nouveau. It was one of the several artistic movements that arose at the end of 19th and the beginning of 20th century in Europe, all showing different traits but all with the goal of breaking from the conservative academic European standards for arts. The Viennese Secession developed an organic style with stylized floral designs and curvilinear patterns, differently from the less geometric and more sinuous style typical of Art Nouveau. The movement had a particular focus on design and architecture, main topics in the issues of Ver Sacrum.

TEXT: FONTS & LAYOUT

The fonts Secession, for titles, and The Golden Type, for textual content, are protagonists of the Secession style on Typetravel.

Secession is an elegant narrow typeface with very high waistlines (Figure X), typical traits of fonts designed by Viennese Secession artists. It is inspired by the font Secession, designed by auer & Company of Stuttgart and H. Berthold AG of Berlin around 1898.

Figure X: Example of title in the issues section

The font Secession also has features in common with several fonts of the time. It resembles the thinner and more geometrical fonts by Charles Rennie Mackintosh, a Scottish designer which influenced the Secession movement (see pages 402 and 403 of the third issue of year 1901) and the geometric shapes designed by Koloman Moser (see Figure X). A further similar alternative to Secession is the font Willow, based on the typeface designed by Mackintosh for the logo of the Willow Tearooms in Glasgow. Secession shows traits in common with many typefaces featured in Ver Sacrum, and in general with fonts designed by Secession artists, as shown in the images below:

Figure X: Ver Sacrum, issue 8 (1899), cover page.

Figure X: Poster for the 17th Vienna Secession designed by Rudolf Jettmar, Austria, 1903 (Victoria & Albert Museum, London)

Figure X: Willow Tea Room logo designed by Charles Rennie Mackintos, Glasgow 1903 (Wikimedia)

Figure X: Ver Sacrum Heft, Joseph Maria Olbrich 1900.

Figure X: Exlibris, Koloman Moser, Fritz Waerndorfer, 1903.

Figure X: Ver Sacrum, issue 11 (1899), cover page.

It was selected among several possible alternatives for its simplicity and readability, as well as for the compatibility and harmony with the font used for the articles content.

Golden Type ITC Std (available here) is the font selected to display the articles content. It was designed by artist William Morris from the Arts and Crafts movement in 1890. It is the font used for the textual content of the magazine Ver Sacrum in the issues between 1898 and 1900. The Golden Type by Morris is an "old style" serif font based on type designed by Nicolas Jenson in Venice around 1470, and it is named after The Golden Legend,supposedly the first book printed using it. The line spacing of the articles was set to 1 to reproduce the text on Ver Sacrum. Below you can see a comparison between the font of Ver Sacrum and the font used in this website's article section:

Figure X: screenshot from Ver Sacrum, issue 10 (1899), page 33

While researching for the exact font, further similar fonts that were taken into consideration are Della Respira, Della Robbia and Doves Type, due to the shape of oblique bars (see lowercase e) and bowls (see lowercase a) and thin terminals.

We now jump from the beginning to the end of the 20th century: 1980s style is the third theme created for Typetravel. This theme aims at reproducing the typical traits of the colorful and eccentric style of this decade. It features contrasting colors, fonts of different sizes and shapes, dark backgrounds, color gradients. In the 1980s, graphic designers were free to manipulate layout, forms, and colors thanks to the introduction of new design software, and were ablo to produce eye-catching results and bold, neon-color typography. Our 1980s aims at reproducing these characteristics, drawing inspiration from different elements of the 1980s culture. Among the several style trends of the decade, the style we reproduced falls under the design category of '80s Digital Style, characterized by the love for scientific and technology-related elements; we also borrowed characterizing elements of the Cyberpunk style, as you will see below.

TEXT: FONTS & LAYOUT

Three fonts were used to reproduce the style of the 1980s: the fonts Arial and Johnny Fever were used for titles, while VCR OSD Mono was selected for textual content.

The image of a VHS videotape (Figure X, left) was used as main reference for the design of the titles. Videotapes entered home use in the 1970s, when the home video industry was created. In the later 1970s and in 1980s, however, a format war involving mainly Betamax and Video Home System took place, and in the 1980s VHS became the world market leader. Furthermore, the home video industry boomed in the 1980s, as the price of VCRs decreased and more and more customers bought them. We thus decided to use VHS as reference due to the relevant role that home video had from the 1980s onwards.

Figure X: Blank VHS Cover (left) used to design the titles of the issues section (right) and on the homepage (below)

Even though the font used on the VHS cover is Helvetica, we selected Arial to display titles (Figure X, right - check out this comparison to see the difference in the design). Arial was released by Monotype Typography in 1982 and became then extremely popular; it was created to be metrically identical to Helvetica, with all character widths identical, so that a document designed in Helvetica could be displayed and printed correctly without having to pay for a Helvetica license.

The last word in each title, however, is represented using the font Johnny Fever (the name is inspired by fictional character of the 1980s American sitcom WKRP in Cincinnati Dr. Johnny Fever). It is a bold cap typeface inspired by Amiga 4k demos. The color bar on the VHS cover (Figure X) was used as background for this font, with the intention of reproducing 1980s titles featuring different font families, weights, sizes and contrasting colors; VHS design was again a major inspiration for this design (Figure X). The same design was used for the homepage title (Figure X)

Figure X: Examples of 1980s graphic design displaying design trends of the decade: neon colors, changing font weights, sizes and families, and color gradients (resources, from left to right: 1, 2, 3, 4, 5)

A graphic element typical of 1980s style is the neon-color perspective grid, usually depicted as a network of glowing straight lines receding in perspective against a black background (Figure X). It is featured in several posters and graphics of the decade (see image 3 and 5 in Figure X), and it is a symbol for 1980s design, in particular for the Digital/Cyberpunk aesthetic - which are the trends represented on Typetravel. Grid designs were featured among others in movies, advertisement, computer animations, arcade games.

Figure X: Three examples of grid designs from the 1980s. Left: Future Life, September, 1980; center: Mattel toys fair catalog, 1982; right: Timerider: The Adventure of Lyle Swann (1982), theatrical poster

The earliest and most relevant examples are from the 1981 computer animation Adam Powers, The Juggler, featuring various glowing grids, and the science fictions movies by Walt Disney Production The Black Hole (1979) and Tron (1982). The Black Hole's opening sequence shows a green laser grid suspended against a dark starscape (Figure X) - the same effect was simulated in a 1980 Selectavision commercial. The movie Tron, on the other hand, is set in the "game grid", which makes up the world itself - a digital universe. The most memorable scene involves the protagonists racing across a grid arena.

Figure X: Frames from the movies Tron (left) and The Black Hole (right)

COLOR SCHEME

The 1980s style features the largest and most diversified color palette. The color scheme on the VHS cover on Figure X was taken as main inspiration for the selection of colors to be applied in the articles section. The use of colors in the design is based on the color correlation of the elements on the page.

Even if vivid neon colors immediately grab the attention of the user's eyes, the prevailing color of this style is #20221F, which results from the addition of a dark gray linear gradient over the background gif displayed in Figure X.

Different colors were used to represent the font: white was chosen for the articles content, inspired by white text on dark background of older screens and chosen to provide a good readability due to the high contrast between text and background.

The color #CD3C37 was sampled by the original Windows 1.0 scrollbar, which was chosen for the color similarity with the dark orange stripe of the VHS design. It was applied to several elements of the page, among others to selections and article abstracts.

Further approximations of the colors in the VHS graphic were used, such as #3d10b9 and #91387a, applied to coloured text shadows, while similar violet gradations were used for other details (e.g. "Hide info" button, floating action button for themes). A lighter violet gradation was used to represent the decorative grid, used also for images captions.

These colors appear all together with #F77811 in the title graphic, while orange is a recurring color used also for hyperlinks.

CONCEPT & LAYOUT

Future is the last theme produced for Typetravel. The user is brought forward in time of 20 years to observe the new trends in web design. In this imaginario, sustainable and environmentally friendly trends have become the rule. Sustainable web design is a priority, so that all actors of the web have took action to give their contribution in preserving the environment. Green web hosting is a fundamental principle: all websites were moved to data centers powered by renewable energy. This, together with the pursue for high level of energy efficiency allows to reduce emissions to a minimum.

With respect to the interface, content findability and usability are priorities and, for this reason, we designed our future website as simple as possible, providing only the minimum amount of content necessary for the fruition, without adding visual elements not useful for the user.

The key asset of this theme is use of the only black color to display the background. Behaind this choice there is the intention of simulating an OLED display. While a traditional light receives electricity through a wire and is emitted by a single source, OLEDs use organic dye between two flat semiconductors to spread the light across a larger space. Their warm, distributed glow is less harsh than bight LEDs thanks to dimmers or added color (learn more here). This technology allows a minor energy consumption and thus a higher efficiency than other technologies with dark and black displays (as shown in Figure X). Furthermore, we expect improvements of this technology in the next decades to confirm the advantages of the use of OLED on displays.
